POSTMODERNISM

c. 1945

Postmodern Classical music was the next evolution in Classical music after Modernism, defined as emerging after the end of World War II. It is characterised by a more eclectic and diverse approach to composition. Unlike Modernist music, which often adheres to strict rules and structures, Postmodern Classical music embraces pastiche, irony, and a blending of various styles and influences. Composers in this genre draw from a wide range of sources, including historical classical works, popular music, and non-Western traditions, creating pieces that often defy conventional categorisation. This music challenges the boundaries between high and low art, questioning the hierarchies and distinctions traditionally upheld in Classical music. By doing so, it reflects broader Postmodern themes such as the rejection of grand narratives and the celebration of multiplicity and ambiguity. Postmodern Classical music invites listeners to reconsider the role of the composer and the performer, fostering a more participatory and interpretive experience.
