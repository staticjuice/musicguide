TEXAS BLUES ROCK


Texas Blues Rock began in the late 70s and early 80s, after Blues Rock ran its course and most major labels had given up on the Blues. As a musical form, Texas Blues had not changed significantly since the mid-60s. The genre involves the introduction of electric guitar and a Rock drum kit, which created a sort of second wave of Blues Rock. This wave happened on a much smaller scale than the first one, but more technically advanced, and with a harder sound. As a result, the music sounded essentially the same, blending classic electrified Chicago and Texas Blues with a distinct Rock influence. As the 80s progressed, Texas Blues Rock found its audience, and it continued to thrive through the late 90s. Typical for this genre is loud, wailing guitars with sustained high notes, and – despite many middle class white artists – this has given rise to the nickname “Ghetto Blues”. A great emphasis on guitar solos, like cries from the ghetto, and a wilder-than-thou sound provide insight as to why, though it is far more likely to encounter Texas Blues Rock and Modern Electric at desolated truck stop bars than in urban ghettos.
