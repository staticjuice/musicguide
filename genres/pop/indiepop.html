INDIE POP


College communities in the US had a sustained culture of amateur Singer-Songwriter during the 70s and 80s. At the end of the 70s, a third lesser known wave of Folk Rock called College Pop emerged, which was more poppy, lo-fi and increasingly amateuristic in sound. During the early 80s, the formation of College Pop into so called “Lo-Fi Pop” is complete. In the same communities there was an interest in Punk Rock’s DIY approach too as well as the brand new Jangle Pop style too. This led to a softer version of the latter, with more focus on melody and less of an abrasive sound harnessing positive, sentimental emotions instead of Indie Rock’s negative angst-filled ones. These artists never achieved the same fame as their Rock companions, but were influential as a whole. Witnessing an unsophisticated, almost rudimentary sound by such an overwhelming amount of artists instilled a great deal of confidence in many Rock and Pop bands to come. Because even though Twee Pop is mostly associated with the late 80s, the genre is still a significant music style, harbouring a vast number of Alternative artists.
