SKA

Ska really put the Jamaican music scene on the map. The nearest American city, New Orleans, brought its own version of R&B to the island where the sound was drastically changed. The R&B rhythm of Bluebeat became accompanied by a marching beat, an influence from Rock & Roll. Although there are African vocals, Ska (like a lot of Jamaican music) is more about the sound and feeling – optimistic, serene and peaceful. Blissful jazzy riffs, horns and staccato guitars are the main tools to achieve this. Ska is also known for its large brass orchestras (10-20 people) dominating the music, whereas bass is rather simplistic with accent on the offbeat. When Philadelphia Soul started to eclipse earlier Soul and Blues, Ska experienced a sharp rise, as the Jamaican public – fond of the Crescent City's music – was forced to take matters in their own hands. This happens simultaneously with the independence of Jamaica in 1962, providing a tremendous opportunity to create a unique and national sound for a new country. Ska becomes a huge success, accompanied by its own jagged, hyperactive type of dancing.
