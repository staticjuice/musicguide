MENTO

1951

All Jamaican music didn't just appear out of thin air of course. A long and diverse culture of European and African Folk music already existed on the island. This slowly led to the first real popular music genre – Mento. Mento was mostly Calypso (a Latin genre from Trinidad & Tobago) with R&B influences. Because Jamaica forms a junction between American, Latin and other music, Mento featured a whole plethora of different instruments, sounding more chaotic than harmonic. The most important thing, however, was the unique lyrics – singers didn't sing but spoke in a rhythmical and improvised way on top of the music, which was called "Toasting". These "toasts" would much later be a major influence on Hip Hop and Drum & Bass. Mento can be regarded as a Folk music genre instead of a popular one, but being the pioneering Jamaican music genre, it makes sense to include it. Mento artists liked to think of themselves as a part of the aristocracy because the country was still a British colony in the 50s. This is shown in nobility titles such as Prince, Lord, Count, Duke and King, which became a bit of a tradition in Jamaican music.
